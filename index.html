Why Crude Isn't $200

~14 Mbd of Gulf exports stopped flowing. After bypass routes and new non-OPEC supply, the net loss hitting refiners is 8.5 Mbd - roughly 8% of global demand. Price should be catastrophic. It isn't. Three things explain the gap. All three are temporary or conditional.

1. Why price hasn't spiked

Most demand was eliminated by state order, not by price. China cut refinery runs by decree. Governments mandated WFH and fuel rationing. Petrochemical plants lost feedstock and shut down. None of this required a high price to happen - which is why Brent is at $105 rather than $200+.

2. Market is pricing in a deal

Brent at ~$105 is below what the physical balance requires if the strait stays closed indefinitely. Traders expect a deal to arrive before inventories hit a critical floor - that expectation compresses the price. If talks fail, price adjusts fast: the no-deal path reaches $130-163 by Aug, given how little price-elasticity oil demand has in the short run.

3. Why this changes over time

Three things are holding price down. All are temporary or conditional:

Inventory draws (~3.4 Mbd): ~550 Mb of emergency-accessible stock left. At this pace, roughly 5 months. When it runs out, there is no substitute - price must rise to destroy demand instead. See Panel 4.
State-directed demand cuts (~5.1 Mbd): sustainable 6-9 months. China's commercial stocks deplete. Developing-Asia subsidy budgets exhaust. As these unwind, the burden shifts to price-driven rationing. See Panels 2-3.
The deal bet: if talks fail, the market's discount to the no-deal fundamental collapses - rapidly. See Panel 3 price paths.

02 · The China Lever

What China's import collapse is actually made of

China cut crude imports ~6 Mbd vs the pre-crisis peak - the single largest demand-side move in the market. Only two pieces are directly observable: refinery run-cuts (~20% state-directed, same slice in Panel 1) and commercial stock draws (from the implied balance). The residual is not directly observable - shown as interpretation.

How long can China sustain this? Probably 6-9 months before hitting hard limits. Commercial stocks (~1 bn bbl, separate from the ~360 Mb government SPR) support ~1.5 Mbd of draws for roughly 20 months. State-directed run cuts are economically painful but politically manageable for several months. Beijing has so far refused to draw the SPR - preserving it as genuine emergency cover. The pressure point comes when commercial stocks approach a ~30-day operating minimum: then the choice is draw the SPR (a significant policy signal) or accept domestic fuel shortages.

China's suppression is also actively capping global prices. If Beijing normalized imports even partially - say, back toward 9 Mbd from 6.6 - OECD draws would accelerate and Brent would spike sharply. That leverage works in both directions and is now a permanent market variable.

03 · Demand Destruction & Price

Rationing by decree, then by price

Three distinct mechanisms destroy demand, each working differently. Petrochemical feedstock cuts are structural and immediate - Gulf naphtha and LPG stopped arriving at the industrial plants (steam crackers) that break those feedstocks into plastics and fertilizer inputs. The plants cut production. Mandated rationing is policy fiat: WFH orders, license-plate systems, government vehicle limits - price-insensitive, deployed first. Price-driven destruction comes last and does the heavy lifting as the others saturate - it rises as Brent climbs.

The dashed suppression line is demand that should have been destroyed but was not: retail price caps (South Korea, Thailand, Philippines, Slovenia) and direct subsidies (Philippines, Thailand, Malaysia, Egypt, Sri Lanka) shield consumers from the price signal. These apply primarily to retail fuel buyers and transport operators in import-dependent Asian economies. The deferred destruction accumulates as fiscal cost and surfaces when subsidies are removed.

Price rises not because the total gap is growing, but because price's share of closing it grows as stock draws and mandated rationing exhaust.

Reading the two Brent paths. The gold line is roughly where the market trades today. Brent at ~$105 is below what the fundamental clearing price would be if the strait stayed closed indefinitely - because traders are betting a deal arrives before inventories hit a critical floor. That bet compresses the price. The dashed red line shows what happens if they're wrong: no deal, risk premium rises, state-directed relief exhausts, and price climbs to ration demand the hard way. The gap between the two lines is not a model range - it is the market's implicit judgment on deal timing.

Elasticity. Formula: P = $70 x (1 + price-rationed volume / 104 Mbd / |eps|) + war-risk premium. The empirically observed eps for the price-driven slice is -0.02 (IEA 1.5 Mbd 2Q cut at $120 avg vs $70 pre-war = (1.5/104)/0.71). But using -0.02 forward produces $200+ no-deal prices, which is implausible if China's state-directed suppression holds. This model uses eps = -0.05 as a structural assumption - higher elasticity as state relief exhausts and adaptation grows - calibrated so the no-deal path lands near Goldman/JPM's $130-175 published range for a sustained closure. The risk premium (the gap between fundamental and market price) is calibrated exactly to observed Mar-May prices. Eps = -0.02, empirically derived from this crisis (see Price Model tab). Two paths: base assumes a deal is struck (risk premium fades, price $125-169); closed assumes no deal (risk premium rises, price $145-224).

The "so what"

If the strait stays closed, three things happen in sequence. First, OECD commercial stocks and the SPR absorb the deficit - but at the current draw rate (~3.4 Mbd) that buffer is roughly 5 months from May. Second, as stocks thin, the balancing burden shifts to price: demand must fall further, and oil's low elasticity means Brent reaches ~$130-163 in the no-deal path (within Goldman/JPM published sustained-closure scenarios). The market currently trades ~$105 because traders expect a deal before inventories hit a critical floor - that expectation is what separates the two price paths. If talks fail and China's state-directed suppression holds, $130-160 is the range. If China suppression also breaks down, higher. Third, every barrel drawn now requires roughly an additional 1 Mbd of supply for 3 years to refill - so reopening does not end the price pressure immediately.

China has been the single largest factor keeping prices below $120: its import collapse freed barrels for the rest of the world. But part of that collapse was stopping a year-long stockpiling program that had itself inflated global demand. Beijing demonstrated it can swing its import slate by ~6 Mbd through direct intervention - in either direction. That is a permanent new market variable.

Where previous models were wrong - and where this one probably is too

Every model published in March-April 2026 predicted Brent above $150 by now. Prices peaked near $144 and have traded $97-110 since. That is not a small miss. Here is what went wrong:

1. Demand flexibility was underestimated

Models used historical short-run price elasticity (~-0.04). But most of the demand destruction was not price-driven - it was state-directed (China run cuts ordered by Beijing) and structural (petrochemical feedstock physically absent). Neither mechanism appears in historical elasticity data. The empirically observed elasticity for the price-driven slice in this crisis is -0.020, but using that as the forward assumption produces implausible $200+ no-deal prices. The right model uses a structural eps of -0.05 (mid-range between the acute-crisis reading and longer-run adaptation) applied only to the price-rationed demand slice, with a separately calibrated risk premium. This produces a no-deal path of $130-163 - within Goldman/JPM published sustained-closure scenarios and more defensible than either extreme.

2. China was modeled as passive

Early models treated China as a price-responsive importer that would slowly reduce consumption. Instead Beijing cut imports by ~6 Mbd in weeks through direct state intervention - a move with no historical precedent at this scale. This single action freed more barrels for the rest of the world than the entire IEA SPR release. No model predicted it; most still don't fully account for China's capacity to move the global balance in either direction at will.

3. The risk premium is news, not fundamentals

Brent fell from $144 to below $100 in the same month - not because the physical balance changed, but because of ceasefire rumors and Trump posts. The geopolitical risk premium is not a stable number: it oscillates with news flow and reprices in hours. All models treat it as a calibrated add-on. None can predict it. This model's forward risk premium is an assumption, not a forecast, and should be read accordingly.

4. Recession feedback was ignored

High oil prices slow GDP. Slower GDP reduces oil demand. Lower demand brings prices down. This feedback loop means the effective demand elasticity rises as prices rise - exactly the opposite of what static models assume. Most forecasts used pre-war GDP baselines. The actual demand contraction (~1.3 Mbd, IEA) is partly price-rationing but also partly GDP destruction from the war itself - trade disruption, capital flight, factory slowdowns - none of which is in the elasticity parameter.

What this model is probably still getting wrong

The forward closed-case price path ($109-142) is likely still too high for the same reasons - it applies elasticity to a residual that state-directed and recession-driven demand flexibility will probably absorb faster than modeled. The inventory draw rate (~3.4 Mbd) is likely already slowing as China's run cuts deepen. The dual-path structure (base vs. closed) captures deal uncertainty but not the intra-week volatility that dominates actual price discovery. Most importantly: this model has no mechanism for the largest single variable - whether and when a deal is struck. That is a geopolitical judgment, not a quantitative one, and it will determine whether prices end 2026 at $90 or $140 more than any supply-demand calculation.

Sources & method

Supply balance. Crude-basis, no double-counting. Identity: net crude supply loss = refinery throughput cut (crude demand destroyed) + inventory draw (residual). Actuals (Mar-May) tie to IEA Oil Market Report (May 13, 2026) April stock decomposition: OECD on-land -4.9 Mbd, non-OECD visible -0.8, oil-on-water +1.8 (a build), netting to -3.9 observed; cumulative Mar-Apr draw ~246 Mb (IEA: -250 Mb). Refinery cut (~5 Mbd, IEA) split China vs. rest-of-world via Kpler run data. Saudi/UAE spare capacity (~4-5 Mbd) excluded as undeliverable while the strait is closed.

Projections (Jun-Aug). Assume the strait STAYS CLOSED - the neutral baseline. The IEA's own published forecast (OMR May 2026) instead assumes gradual reopening from 3Q26, which would shrink the deficit faster. Pentagon internal assessment: worst-case closure 6+ months.

China decomposition. Import collapse split into: (1) refinery run-cuts - observable via run data; (2) commercial stock draws - observable via implied supply-demand balance; (3) residual - imports fell more than (1)+(2) explain, shown as interpretation. "Over-buying" is inferred from the balance, not directly measured. Monthly sub-split is structured inference consistent with published aggregates (~1 Mbd surplus barrels set aside; ~4+ Mbd deficit met via runs/inventories/consumption).

Price model. Brent is derived in two components, not assumed:

Fundamental clearing price: uses the constant short-run price elasticity of demand formula. If the market requires Q Mbd of price-driven demand rationing to clear the residual gap (after refinery cuts, mandated rationing, and stock draws absorb the rest), then the required price is:

P_fund = P0 x (1 + (Q / D) / |eps|)
where P0 = $70 (pre-crisis Brent baseline), D = 104 Mbd (global demand), eps = -0.05 (structural mid-range). Empirically observed acute-crisis eps = -0.02 (IEA 1.5 Mbd 2Q cut / 71% price rise), but this understates forward elasticity as state-directed cuts exhaust and price adaptation grows. -0.05 is the compromise. Risk premium calibrated to observed actuals ($18/$42/$23 for Mar/Apr/May); forward: base ~$15-20 (deal expected), no-deal ~$40-55 (war risk persists). Because eps is small, even a modest uncleared residual requires a large price rise - this is why Brent rose sharply on a relatively small physical gap.

Geopolitical risk premium: Added on top of the fundamental; calibrated to match observed Brent in Mar-May (modeled fundamental $75-85 vs. observed $85-120, implying a $10-35/bbl risk premium from the war/closure itself). Forward risk premium held at ~$22-40/bbl in the closed-case path.

Price sensitivity: at eps = -0.03 (less elastic) prices are ~10% higher; at eps = -0.06, ~10% lower.

Drawable-stock runway. Uses the emergency-accessible buffer above safe minimum operating levels (~1.1 bn bbl at crisis start, per OPEC MOMR analysis and IEA). Runway shown at constant May draw rate (3.4 Mbd) for transparency - actual draws moderate as demand destruction deepens, which extends the runway but raises price instead. Excludes stranded oil-on-water (building, not accessible). Decision-support visual, not investment advice.
